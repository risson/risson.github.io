This quarter our objective is to analyze on students' dropout. We want to understand what causes our students to dropout. If possible, predict when our students are going to dropout, in order to understand their lifetime value. As a first step, we would like to look at whether our students are leaving us faster than before. I understand that survival analysis is a great tool to do this kind of analysis. [This blog](http://daynebatten.com) is a great place for you to learn more about survival analysis (especially posts from 2016 to 2017). In this post, I'll demo how I use it. ## 1. CustomerKey CustomerStatus CustomerSubStatus CourseMemberKey CourseStatus StartDate EndDate Age
001 ExStudent Cancle CM001 Finish 2015-12-21 2016-02-23 7
001 ExStudent Cancle CM102 Dropout 2016-03-30 2016-06-30 8
002 Student Studying CM001 Finish 2015-12-21 2016-02-23 7

We need to re-organize the data set in order to match the format that the R package for survival analysis can understand (df2):

Note that each customer can take several courses. Each course has it's status. Each customer has a status and sub status. We only capture the last customer status and customer sub status. Historical customer status are not kept for every course a customer takes. We will not mark a customer as dropout as long as his/her customer status is student. ## 2. Verify finding and make changes Wait, as a professional data scientist, we need to be careful when showing insights to senior management group. Is there anything wrong in the process we've done? Actually yes. When we look at the group 'Joined After 2016', we found that people can sign up from 2016, or early 2017. Note that we captured data till April 14, 2017. For those people who signed up in April 2017, clearly they only 'survived' for less than 1 month, no matter they really dropped out or not. Compared with those who signed up from before 2016, total amount of survivals must always be higher. So, we have to make changes. We look at survival within 300 days. Also, we remove students who signed up after April 14, 2016. ``` df2 <- df1[SignUpDate<=as.Date('2016-4-14')] ``` Let's plot again: ``` fit <- survfit(Surv(Days, Dropout) ~ SignUpAFT2016, data = df2) # Plot the results, add a legend, add a title plot(fit, lty = 1:2, mark.time = FALSE, xlim=c(0,300), ylim=c(.75,1), xlab = 'Days since Joining', ylab = 'Percent Surviving') legend(10, 0.8, c('Joined Before 2016', 'Joined After 2016'), lty=1:2, bty = 'n', ncol = 2) title(main = "EF Survival Curves by Enroll Date (before vs. after 2016)") ``` Now the survival curve looks much more reasonable. It seems like our 5 month retention rate is better than before, but retention for more than 160 days seems worse. This means we need to dig out reasons why retention for longer period is worse than before. Is it because we set up stricter rules on short term retention? Did we launch new course products for which our students didn't like that much? Those questions will lead us to create new analytics projects.

1. Reshape data set

2. Create Survival Curve

# Fit survival curves based on the sign up time binary
fit <- survfit(Surv(Days, Churn) ~ SignUpAFT2016, data = df1)

# Plot the results, add a legend, add a title
plot(fit, lty = 1:2, mark.time = FALSE, xlim=c(0,600), ylim=c(.5,1), xlab = 'Days since Joining', ylab = 'Percent Surviving')
legend(10, 0.6, c('Joined Before 2016', 'Joined After 2016'), lty=1:2, bty = 'n', ncol = 2)

The plot looks like this:

It seems like dropout rate is getting higher after 2016 than before. If we show this result to the C-suite, they must be shocked!

3. Verify finding and make changes

Wait, as a professional data scientist, we need to be careful when showing insights to senior management group. Is there anything wrong in the process we've done?

Actually yes. When we look at the group 'Joined After 2016', we found that people can sign up from 2016, or early 2017. Note that we captured data till April 14, 2017. For those people who signed up in April 2017, clearly they only 'survived' for less than 1 month, no matter they really dropped out or not. Compared with those who signed up from before 2016, total amount of survivals must always be higher.

So, we have to make changes. We look at survival within 300 days. Also, we remove students who signed up after April 14, 2016.

df2 <- df1[SignUpDate<=as.Date('2016-4-14')]

Let's plot again:

fit <- survfit(Surv(Days, Dropout) ~ SignUpAFT2016, data = df2)

# Plot the results, add a legend, add a title
plot(fit, lty = 1:2, mark.time = FALSE, xlim=c(0,300), ylim=c(.75,1), xlab = 'Days since Joining', ylab = 'Percent Surviving')
legend(10, 0.8, c('Joined Before 2016', 'Joined After 2016'), lty=1:2, bty = 'n', ncol = 2)
title(main = "EF Survival Curves by Enroll Date (before vs. after 2016)")
